THE DESIGNER’S GUIDE TO TUNGSTEN CARBIDE
Design Recommendations to Avoid Braze Strains
Since braze strains result from the diﬀerence in the thermal expansion rates of the materials
being brazed, it follows that the use of materials with similar thermal expansion coeﬃcients
would result in less braze strains being introduced into the assembly.
Heavy tungsten alloy has a coeﬃcient of thermal expansion approximately equal to that
of cemented carbide.
The material wets well with silver ﬁller material, producing very sound
brazed joints with minimum braze strain.
Heavy tungsten alloy has physical properties similar
to those of mild steel and is easily machined.
If the application being considered can employ heavy tungsten alloy, as depicted above for a
grinder spindle housing shown in Figure IV-6, the probability for success is enhanced.
The two
materials, having similar expansion and contraction curves, will minimize the braze strains
in the assembly.
Using a series of short pieces of carbide instead of one larger piece will reduce the cumulative
eﬀect of braze strain.
An assembly that is designed with joints at non-critical points will avoid
strain at highly stressed areas and reduce the likelihood of brazing failure or cracking.
Strains can often be relieved on a mis-proportioned assembly by brazing only on one surface.
A special mask-like paint can be applied to the surface on which no attachment is needed so
that the braze alloy will not wet those surfaces.
A relief gap, machined into the body, will also
help to prevent braze material from bonding all surfaces.
